## The Short Answer

AI onboarding flows in 2025 follow three distinct patterns: zero onboarding (ChatGPT, Claude), contextual onboarding (Perplexity, Notion AI), and example-driven onboarding (Midjourney, DALL-E). The best approach depends on product complexity — simple AI products need minimal onboarding, while complex AI products need guided discovery. The common thread across all successful AI onboarding is that they prioritize action over explanation: users learn by doing, not by reading documentation.

---

## Example 1: ChatGPT — Zero Onboarding

**Pattern: No onboarding**

ChatGPT has no onboarding flow. There's no tutorial, no feature tour, no welcome screen. You open the app and see an empty input field. That's it.

**What this means for design:** Zero onboarding works when the product's primary action is obvious. A single input field communicates its purpose without explanation. ChatGPT's design assumes that users understand what an AI chatbot does because they've seen them before.

**Strengths:**
- No friction — users can start immediately
- No cognitive load — one input field is trivial to understand
- No learning curve — the interaction model is universal (text input)

**Weaknesses:**
- Advanced features (code interpreter, file uploads, browsing) are poorly discovered
- Users who need structured guidance have to find tutorials elsewhere
- No way to communicate important product changes or updates

**Best for:** AI products with a single, obvious primary action.

---

## Example 2: Claude — Progressive Contextual Onboarding

**Pattern: Onboarding through usage**

Claude starts with a simple interface but introduces features progressively. The file upload button appears when you're likely to need it. The multi-tab interface is discoverable but not forced. The image analysis feature is accessible but not highlighted.

**What this means for design:** Claude's approach balances simplicity with discoverability. New users see a clean interface. Power users can discover advanced features through exploration. The design assumes that users will find features organically rather than through explicit guidance.

**Strengths:**
- Clean interface for new users
- Advanced features accessible through exploration
- No feature tour that interrupts first use

**Weaknesses:**
- Some features are hard to discover without prior knowledge
- Power users want more control over feature visibility
- No way to signal that advanced features exist

**Best for:** AI products with multiple capabilities that serve different user segments.

---

## Example 3: Midjourney — Example-Driven Onboarding

**Pattern: Show, don't tell**

Midjourney's onboarding is entirely example-driven. When you first access the platform, you see a gallery of AI-generated images. There's no explanation of how to generate images — you learn by looking at what others have created and experimenting with prompts.

**What this means for design:** Example-driven onboarding works for visual and creative AI products because users can understand capability through demonstration. Seeing a high-quality image makes users want to create something similar, which drives them to experiment with prompts.

**Strengths:**
- Inspires users immediately through visual examples
- No text-based explanation required
- Encourages experimentation through visual feedback

**Weaknesses:**
- Not intuitive for non-visual users
- Requires understanding of prompt-based interaction
- Difficult for users who don't know what they want to create

**Best for:** Creative AI products where visual output is the primary value.

---

## Example 4: Perplexity AI — Feature-Gated Discovery

**Pattern: Unlock features through usage**

Perplexity AI starts with a simple search interface but progressively introduces features as you use the product. The "Sources" panel appears on your first query. The "Pro" features are introduced through usage, not through a tutorial. The related questions feature emerges organically as you build a research session.

**What this means for design:** Perplexity's approach treats onboarding as an ongoing process rather than a one-time event. Features are revealed through natural usage patterns, which means users discover them at the right time rather than before they're needed.

**Strengths:**
- Features appear when users are most likely to use them
- No upfront complexity
- Natural progression from basic to advanced usage

**Weaknesses:**
- Some features require multiple interactions to discover
- New users may not realize what the product can do
- No clear roadmap of capabilities

**Best for:** AI products where features are discovered naturally through usage patterns.

---

## Example 5: Notion AI — Integration-Based Onboarding

**Pattern: AI as a feature, not a separate product**

Notion AI doesn't have a traditional onboarding flow because it's not a separate product — it's integrated into Notion. Users discover AI through slash commands and inline suggestions. The onboarding is contextual: AI appears when you're editing text, creating content, or working on documents.

**What this means for design:** Integration-based onboarding works when AI is a feature of an existing product rather than a standalone product. The onboarding is the product itself — users discover AI through their existing workflows.

**Strengths:**
- AI is discoverable within existing workflows
- No separate learning curve for a new product
- Contextual triggers for AI features

**Weaknesses:**
- Users may not know AI exists
- AI features can feel buried in the product
- Limited control over AI visibility and prominence

**Best for:** AI features integrated into existing products.

## The Onboarding Patterns for AI Products

Based on these 5 examples, here are the onboarding patterns that work for AI products in 2025:

### Pattern 1: Zero Onboarding
**When to use:** The primary action is obvious (single input field, simple command)
**Examples:** ChatGPT, basic AI chatbots
**Design principle:** Don't explain what's obvious.

### Pattern 2: Progressive Contextual
**When to use:** Multiple features that serve different use cases
**Examples:** Claude, most AI assistant products
**Design principle:** Introduce features through usage, not documentation.

### Pattern 3: Example-Driven
**When to use:** Visual or creative AI products
**Examples:** Midjourney, DALL-E, image generation tools
**Design principle:** Show what's possible, don't explain how it works.

### Pattern 4: Feature-Gated Discovery
**When to use:** Products where features are discovered through natural usage
**Examples:** Perplexity, research-focused AI tools
**Design principle:** Let usage reveal capability.

### Pattern 5: Integration-Based
**When to use:** AI integrated into existing products
**Examples:** Notion AI, Figma AI, Microsoft Copilot
**Design principle:** Make AI discoverable within existing workflows.

## What All Successful AI Onboarding Has in Common

1. **Action over explanation** — Users learn by doing, not by reading. Every example starts with a prompt, not a tutorial.

2. **Immediate value** — Users see a result within seconds of starting, whether it's a generated image, a chat response, or a research summary.

3. **Progressive complexity** — The interface starts simple and reveals more as users engage. No product forces complexity on day one.

4. **No technical jargon** — Onboarding flows don't explain how the AI works. They show what the AI can do. Users don't need to know about tokens, context windows, or model versions.

5. **Organic discovery** — Features are discovered through exploration, not through forced tutorials. The best AI onboarding makes users curious enough to explore.

---

## The Bottom Line

AI onboarding in 2025 has converged on five distinct patterns, each suited to a different product type. The key insight is that the best AI onboarding doesn't feel like onboarding at all — it feels like the product working. Users shouldn't need to learn a new interface. They should be able to use the product immediately and discover its capabilities through natural exploration.

If your AI product has an onboarding flow, ask yourself: would the product work just as well without it? If the answer is yes, you've already got the right approach.
