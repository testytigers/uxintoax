## The Short Answer

AI company UX interviews focus on three areas: how you design for AI uncertainty, how you approach AI product design, and how you collaborate with AI teams. Here are 20 real questions with model answers to help you prepare.

---

## Part 1: AI Design Philosophy

### 1. How do you design for AI uncertainty?

**Model answer:** "I design for uncertainty by providing clear feedback states — loading, thinking, done, and error. I use progressive disclosure to manage user expectations about what the AI can and can't do. I also design for correction — giving users the ability to edit, retry, or provide feedback when the AI output is wrong. Trust is built through transparency about what the AI is doing and what it might get wrong."

### 2. How is AX design different from traditional UX?

**Model answer:** "Traditional UX focuses on predictable interactions — users click, the system responds immediately. AX design has to handle unpredictable AI behavior, variable response quality, and asynchronous interactions. The core difference is that in AX, the system isn't just responding to inputs — it's making decisions. That requires designing for trust, transparency, and user control over AI decisions."

### 3. What does 'designing for trust' mean in the context of AI products?

**Model answer:** "Trust in AI products means users believe the AI is competent, reliable, and acting in their best interest. I design for trust through: (1) transparency — showing users how the AI is making decisions, (2) consistency — delivering reliable results, (3) accountability — giving users control and the ability to correct errors, and (4) appropriate confidence signaling — never over-promising on what the AI can do."

## Part 2: Practical AI Design

### 4. Walk me through your process for designing an AI feature.

**Model answer:** "I start by understanding the AI capability — what can the model do, what are its limitations? Then I map the user journey, identifying where AI adds value and where it might create friction. I prototype the interaction flow, focusing on feedback states and error handling. I test with real AI outputs (not mockups) because AI behavior is unpredictable. Finally, I define success metrics — accuracy, user satisfaction, and task completion rate."

### 5. How do you handle AI hallucinations in your designs?

**Model answer:** "I design for hallucinations by: (1) setting expectations — being clear about what the AI might get wrong, (2) providing verification — allowing users to check facts, (3) offering correction — making it easy for users to fix errors, and (4) logging hallucinations — working with the product team to identify patterns and improve the model. The key is never pretending the AI is infallible."

### 6. How do you design prompt interfaces?

**Model answer:** "Prompt design is conversation design. I start by understanding the user's mental model — how do they think about the problem? Then I design prompt patterns that match that mental model. I use progressive disclosure to help users learn what the AI can do, provide examples to set expectations, and include prompts that guide users toward effective queries. I also design for iteration — most users refine their prompts, not get it right the first time."

## Part 3: Collaboration and Process

### 7. How do you collaborate with AI engineers?

**Model answer:** "I partner closely with AI engineers from day one. I bring user research insights that inform model capabilities and limitations. They bring technical knowledge about what's possible. We jointly define success metrics, prototype with real AI outputs, and iterate based on user testing. The best collaborations happen when designers understand the AI tech stack and engineers understand the user experience."

### 8. How do you evaluate whether AI is the right solution?

**Model answer:** "I ask three questions: (1) Is there a simpler solution that doesn't require AI? (2) Will users understand and trust the AI output? (3) Is there enough data to build a reliable model? If the answer to any of these is 'no,' AI isn't the right solution. I advocate for simplicity first and reserve AI for problems where it adds clear value."

### 9. How do you measure success for AI design?

**Model answer:** "I track: (1) Task completion rate — can users achieve their goals? (2) User satisfaction — measured through surveys and interviews, (3) Error rate — how often does the AI get things wrong? (4) Time on task — is AI making things faster or slower? (5) Trust metrics — do users rely on the AI's recommendations? The specific metrics depend on the product, but these five cover the key dimensions."

## Part 4: Scenario-Based Questions

### 10. A stakeholder wants to add AI to a feature where it doesn't add value. How do you handle it?

**Model answer:** "I explain the cost-benefit analysis — what value does AI add, and what's the cost of implementing it? If AI doesn't improve the user experience or business outcome, I advocate for a simpler solution. I use data from user research to support my position. If the stakeholder insists, I prototype both the AI and non-AI versions and test them with users."

### 11. Users are not trusting your AI feature. What do you do?

**Model answer:** "First, I diagnose the trust issue. Is it a competence problem (the AI gets things wrong)? A transparency problem (users don't understand how it works)? Or an ethical problem (users are uncomfortable with AI making decisions)? Then I design solutions: improving accuracy, adding transparency, or adjusting the AI's role. I also communicate the changes to users."

### 12. You're designing for a domain you're unfamiliar with (e.g., healthcare AI). How do you approach it?

**Model answer:** "I start with research — I interview domain experts, study existing products, and understand the regulatory constraints. I don't pretend to know more than I do. I rely on user research to understand the specific needs and risks of the domain. I partner with subject matter experts to validate my designs. The unfamiliarity actually helps — I bring fresh perspective that domain experts might not have."

## Part 5: Career and Growth

### 13. Why do you want to work on AI products?

**Model answer:** "I believe AI is the biggest design challenge of our generation. We're designing interfaces for systems that don't follow predictable rules, that make their own decisions, and that will fundamentally change how humans work. It's the most exciting design problem I've encountered, and I want to be part of building AI products that are helpful, transparent, and trustworthy."

### 14. What's your experience with AI tools?

**Model answer:** "I use AI tools in my design process for research synthesis, content generation, and prototyping. I'm familiar with ChatGPT for UX writing, Claude for research analysis, and Figma AI for design tasks. I also experiment with AI-powered tools for user research, data analysis, and design exploration. My experience is practical — I use AI tools daily to improve my design output."

### 15. How do you stay current on AI design trends?

**Model answer:** "I follow AI design thought leaders on LinkedIn, read research papers from top AI labs, attend AI design conferences, and participate in AI design communities. I also experiment with new AI tools regularly. The AI field moves fast, so I dedicate 30 minutes daily to learning and 2–3 hours weekly to experimenting with new tools."

## Part 6: Quick Questions

### 16. What's the biggest misconception about AI products?
"AI is not magic. It's a tool with specific capabilities and limitations. Good designers understand those boundaries and design accordingly."

### 17. How do you design for AI errors?
"Clear error messages, recovery options, and user education about when errors might occur."

### 18. What makes a good AI agent?
"Competence, transparency, accountability, and appropriate personality."

### 19. How do you handle bias in AI design?
"Test with diverse user groups, audit AI outputs for bias, and design for user correction."

### 20. What's the future of UX design?
"Converging with AX design. The boundary between traditional UX and AI design will blur as AI becomes integral to every product."

## FAQ

### How should I prepare for an AI company interview?
Study AI design principles, practice with AI tools, review AI product case studies, and prepare specific examples from your experience.

### What's the most important AI design skill?
Understanding AI limitations. Knowing what AI can and can't do is more valuable than knowing how to use AI tools.

### Should I learn to code for AI design interviews?
Not required, but helpful. Understanding the basics of how AI models work is more important than coding ability.

### How many AI projects should I have in my portfolio?
At least one. Even a personal project that demonstrates AI design thinking is enough to show you're serious about AX.
