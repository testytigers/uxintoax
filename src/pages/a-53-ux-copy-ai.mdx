## The Short Answer

Writing UX copy for AI products is different from regular UX copy because you're writing for probabilistic, dynamic interfaces. The copy needs to set expectations about AI capabilities, guide prompt-based interaction, and handle errors gracefully. The best tone is confident, helpful, and honest about limitations.

---

## The Core Challenge: Uncertainty

Traditional UX copy deals with certainty — "Click here to save." "Your changes have been saved." "Form submitted successfully."

AI UX copy deals with uncertainty — "Generating your response..." "This might not be what you need, but here's another option..." "I'm not sure about this, but here's my best attempt."

Writing for uncertainty is harder than writing for certainty. You need to balance honesty with confidence, and guidance with brevity.

---

## Writing Principles for AI Copy

### Principle 1: Set Expectations

Before users interact with AI, they need to know what to expect.

**Examples:**
- "AI generates text based on your prompts. Results vary — always review before using."
- "This feature uses AI to suggest design improvements. Suggestions are not guaranteed to be useful."
- "AI can make mistakes. Always verify AI-generated content."

### Principle 2: Be Honest About Limitations

Don't over-promise AI capabilities. Be honest about what the AI can and can't do.

**Bad:** "AI will write your entire essay."

**Good:** "AI can help you draft content. You'll still need to review and edit it."

### Principle 3: Guide Prompt-Based Interaction

AI interfaces rely on prompts. The copy needs to help users write effective prompts.

**Examples:**
- "Describe what you want the AI to create. Be specific for better results."
- "Try prompts like: 'Write a product description for a project management tool' or 'Summarize this research paper'."
- "Need help? Try: 'Give me 5 ideas for a blog post about UX design'."

### Principle 4: Handle Errors Gracefully

AI makes mistakes. The copy needs to acknowledge errors without losing trust.

**Examples:**
- "I couldn't generate that response. Try rephrasing your prompt."
- "This output doesn't seem right. Would you like me to try again?"
- "I'm not sure I understood correctly. Can you clarify what you need?"

## Tone Guidelines

### The AI Persona

Your AI should have a consistent personality that matches your product. Define the persona before writing any copy.

**Elements of AI personality:**
- Tone (professional, friendly, playful)
- Level of formality (formal, casual)
- Response style (direct, conversational)
- Confidence level (humble, confident, cautious)

**Example AI personas:**
- Professional: "I can help you create a project plan. Here's a draft based on your requirements."
- Friendly: "Let me help with that! Here's a project plan draft I put together."
- Playful: "On it! Here's your project plan. Try not to be amazed."

---

### Error Message Tone

Error messages should be honest, helpful, and empathetic.

**Bad:** "An error occurred. Please try again."

**Good:** "I couldn't generate that response. This can happen when the AI is unsure about what you're asking. Try rephrasing your prompt or being more specific."

---

### Prompt Suggestion Tone

Prompt suggestions should be encouraging and practical.

**Bad:** "Enter your prompt here."

**Good:** "Describe what you want to create. Be specific for better results. Try: 'Write a product description for a project management tool.'"

---

## Writing for Specific AI Interfaces

### Chat Interfaces

Chat copy needs to be conversational and concise.

**Guidelines:**
- Keep responses under 3-4 sentences for most interactions
- Use clear, simple language
- Avoid jargon
- End with questions or suggestions when appropriate

### Command Interfaces

Command copy needs to be brief and clear.

**Guidelines:**
- Use action verbs
- Keep suggestions under 10 words
- Be specific about what the command does

### Dashboard Interfaces

Dashboard copy needs to be informative and actionable.

**Guidelines:**
- Use clear headings
- Provide context for AI-generated data
- Offer clear next steps

---

## The AI Copy Checklist

Before publishing any AI interface copy, ask:
1. Does it set clear expectations about AI capabilities?
2. Is it honest about limitations?
3. Does it guide users effectively?
4. Is it clear and concise?
5. Does it handle errors gracefully?
6. Is the tone consistent with the product personality?
7. Would a non-technical user understand this?

---

## The Bottom Line

Writing UX copy for AI products is different from regular UX copy because you're writing for uncertainty, probabilistic output, and prompt-based interaction. The key principles are: set expectations, be honest about limitations, guide prompt-based interaction, and handle errors gracefully.

If you're writing copy for an AI product, start by defining the AI's personality, then write copy that matches that personality while being honest about what the AI can and can't do.
