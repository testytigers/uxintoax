## The Short Answer

You get your first AX design job by building a portfolio that proves you can do the work, not by waiting for a job title that says "AX Designer." The companies hiring for AX skills right now are mostly calling the role something else — AI Product Designer, Conversational Designer, UX Researcher for AI, Product Designer with AI focus. Learn to spot the role, build the portfolio, and apply.

---

## Why "No Experience" Doesn't Matter for AX

Here's the reality: AX design is so new that almost nobody has "AX Designer" on their resume. The people doing the work right now were called Product Designers, UX Researchers, Interaction Designers, or UI Designers six months ago. The job title hasn't caught up to the work.

What companies actually care about right now is whether you can:

- Design interfaces for unpredictable, autonomous systems
- Communicate effectively with AI teams
- Think about user experience in a world where the system makes its own decisions

You can demonstrate all of these without having held the title. In fact, your lack of AX-specific experience can be an advantage — you bring fresh perspective that people who've been doing "traditional" UX for years don't have.

## Step 1: Build a Portfolio Piece That Proves AX Thinking

You don't need a real job to show AX skills. You need three portfolio pieces that demonstrate you understand the core challenges.

### Piece 1: Redesign an Existing AI Product's UX

Pick any AI product that has clear UX problems — ChatGPT's interface, Perplexity's answer format, an AI chatbot from a bank or insurance company. Write a professional critique identifying:

- Where the AI's behavior creates confusing interactions
- What feedback states are missing (thinking, error, uncertainty)
- How trust could be improved
- What a better experience would look like

Include screenshots or mockups. The point isn't to produce production-ready designs — it's to demonstrate that you see AX-specific problems that traditional UX designers miss.

### Piece 2: Design an AI Feature from Scratch

Pick a non-AI product and imagine what an AI-powered version of a key feature would look like. For example:

- How would Duolingo's learning path work with an AI tutor?
- What would a fitness app's workout recommendation interface look like when powered by an AI agent?
- How would a banking app handle an AI financial advisor?

Your design should show:
- How the AI communicates its reasoning
- How the user can correct or override the AI
- How the system handles uncertainty or errors
- How trust and transparency are built into the interaction

### Piece 3: Document an AI Design Process

Write a case study that shows your process for designing an AI feature. Cover:

- How you defined the AI's capabilities and limitations
- How you identified where AI adds value vs where it creates friction
- How you tested with real AI outputs (not just mockups)
- What you learned from user testing

This proves you have a methodology, not just good design skills. Companies hiring for AX roles want people who can think through the problem, not just make things look nice.

## Step 2: Learn the Skills Employers Actually Want

You don't need a degree or certification. You need to understand these concepts deeply enough to discuss them confidently in an interview:

### AI Capability Awareness
Understand what LLMs can do, what they can't do, and what they're currently bad at. Read about hallucinations, token limits, context windows, and prompt engineering. You don't need to build AI — you need to understand what you're designing for.

### Conversation Design Basics
Learn the fundamentals of designing text and voice conversations. Study dialogue flows, error recovery patterns, and how to design for multi-turn interactions. The best resources are practical — build a chatbot interface and learn through doing.

### AI Product Thinking
Understand what makes AI products different from traditional software products. The key difference: traditional products have predictable behavior, AI products have variable behavior. Design for that variability. Study products that handle uncertainty well (and badly).

### Basic Technical Literacy
You don't need to code, but you should understand the AI tech stack at a conceptual level: what's a model, what's a prompt, what's an API, what's fine-tuning. This helps you communicate with engineers and understand what's realistically possible.

## Step 3: Make Your Existing Experience Speak to AX

Most UX designers already have relevant experience — they just haven't labeled it as AX. Go through your past projects and identify where AI-related skills apply:

- Did you design for error states? That's relevant to AI error handling.
- Did you work on onboarding flows? That translates to AI agent onboarding.
- Did you write UX copy? That's directly applicable to prompt design and AI communication.
- Did you do user research? Research methods transfer directly to evaluating AI products.

Reframe your resume and portfolio to highlight these connections. You're not starting from zero — you're transitioning.

## Step 4: Where to Find AX Jobs When Nobody Uses That Title

The job titles you'll actually see on job boards for AX work include:

- **AI Product Designer** — the most common label right now
- **Conversational Designer** — older term, still used
- **UX Designer, AI/ML Team** — many AI companies list this
- **Interaction Designer, AI** — common at research-heavy companies
- **Product Designer, Intelligent Systems** — used by companies building AI agents
- **UX Researcher, AI Products** — research roles on AI teams
- **Design Lead, AI** — senior roles at startups
- **Prompt Designer** — emerging title at some companies

Search for these titles on LinkedIn, Wellfound (for startups), and company career pages directly. The companies hiring for these roles right now are:

1. **AI-native companies** — OpenAI, Anthropic, Perplexity, Cursor, Replit, Notion AI
2. **AI teams at established companies** — Google, Microsoft, Apple, Amazon, Meta
3. **AI startups** — hundreds of them, check Wellfound and Y Combinator's job board
4. **Consulting agencies** — agencies building AI products for clients

## Step 5: The Application Strategy That Works

### Apply broadly but strategically
Apply to 10-15 roles per week. Don't spam — tailor each application to show you understand the company's AI product. Reference specific features, mention what you think could be improved, and explain why you're excited about their specific approach to AI.

### Build in public
Share your portfolio pieces on LinkedIn and Twitter. Write threads about AI design challenges. Post your critiques of AI products. Companies notice designers who publicly engage with AI design problems — it proves you're passionate and knowledgeable.

### Network with people doing the work
Find AX designers, AI Product Designers, and Conversational Designers on LinkedIn. Send short, specific messages — not "let's connect" but "I read your article/post about X and had a question about Y." People respond to specific, thoughtful outreach.

### Consider contract or freelance work
Many AI companies hire designers on a contract basis before converting to full-time. This is actually the fastest path — you get real experience, build credibility, and often transition to a permanent role.
