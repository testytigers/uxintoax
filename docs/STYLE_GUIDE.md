# uxintoax.com — Editorial Style Guide

> The voice, rules, and standards for every word published on uxintoax.com.
> This document is the single source of truth. If a writer doesn't know how to write something, they read this guide.

---

## 1. VOICE & TONE

### Our Voice (5 adjectives)

1. **Direct** — We say what we mean. No hedging, no padding, no "perhaps" or "maybe."
2. **Confident** — We write from authority. We don't say "some people think" — we take a position and back it up.
3. **Forward-looking** — We're writing about what's coming, not what was. Our default lens is 12–24 months ahead.
4. **Data-informed** — Every opinion is backed by evidence. If we can't cite data, we say so explicitly.
5. **Practical** — We don't theorize. Every article gives readers something they can use on Monday morning.

### What We Never Are (5 adjectives)

1. **Academic** — We're not writing for journals or conferences. This is for working designers.
2. **Promotional** — We're not sponsored. We're not press releases. If a tool has weaknesses, we name them.
3. **Hedged** — "It depends" is the enemy. We take a stance, then qualify it with nuance.
4. **Buzzwordy** — We don't use words to sound smart. We use words to make people think.
5. **Condescending** — Our readers are professionals. We don't explain things they don't need explained. We don't oversimplify.

### Example Sentences That Sound Like Us

- *"AI won't replace UX designers. It will replace UX designers who treat AI as a novelty instead of a new design medium."*
- *"The median salary for an AX-designated role is $148,000 in the US. But the title itself is rare — the skills command that salary whether the job posting uses the label or not."*
- *"ChatGPT's interface isn't bad. It's just optimized for engagement, not for the kind of work UX designers do."*

### Example Sentences That Don't Sound Like Us

- *"In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape, it is important to consider the multifaceted nature of AI in the context of user experience design."*
- *"Many experts believe that AI may potentially impact the future of design in various ways."*
- *"This article will explore the various aspects of prompt design and its relationship to the broader field of UX."*

### Sentence Rules

- **Target average sentence length:** 15–20 words. Use longer sentences (25–30) for technical explanations. Never exceed 40.
- **Technical jargon:** Define on first use. Never assume familiarity with LLM, agentic, zero-shot, or RAG. A UX designer should be able to read every article without Googling terms.
- **First person vs second person:** Use "you" (second person) to address the reader. Use "I" (first person) sparingly — only for strong opinions or personal anecdotes. Never use "we" to mean "the author" — that's pretentious.
- **Opening articles:** Never start with "In this article..." or "This post covers..." Start with a statement, a number, a contradiction, or a scenario. The first sentence must earn the second.
- **Closing articles:** Never end with "In conclusion." End with an actionable takeaway, a bold prediction, or a question that pushes the reader to the next step. Always include the newsletter CTA at the bottom.

## 2. FORMATTING RULES

### Headings

- **H1:** Article title only. One per page.
- **H2:** Major sections. Write them as complete phrases or questions, not fragment labels.
  - ✅ `What is Agent Experience Design?`
  - ❌ `Definition`
- **H3:** Sub-sections. Keep them tight. No more than 3 H3s per H2.
- **H4+:** Avoid. If you need H4s, the section is too complex. Break it up.

### Lists vs Prose

- **Use bullet lists when:**
  - You have 3+ items of equal weight
  - The items are scannable on their own
  - You're listing tools, steps, or criteria
- **Use prose when:**
  - The connection between items matters
  - You're telling a story or explaining a concept
  - The items aren't parallel (different length, different weight)
- **Use numbered lists when:**
  - Order matters (steps, rankings, timelines)
  - You explicitly reference the numbers later in the article

### Meta Descriptions

- **Length:** 140–160 characters
- **Format:** [Subject] + [Action/Value] + [Differentiator]
- **Example:** *"Agent Experience (AX) design is the practice of designing how humans interact with autonomous AI agents. Learn the skills, tools, and career paths."*
- **Never** start with "Welcome to..." or "Discover..." or "Learn about..."

### Title Tags

- **Length:** 50–60 characters
- **Format:** [Keyword phrase] + [Year/Modifier] (optional)
- **Example:** `What is Agent Experience Design? | UX into AX`
- **Never** include "blog" or "post" or "article" in the title tag.

### Call-to-Action Language

- Use action verbs: *Subscribe, Start, Build, Read, Try, Download, Apply*
- Never use soft CTAs: *Feel free to..., You might want to..., If you're interested...*
- The newsletter CTA is always: *"Get the weekly AX brief. One email per week. No fluff."*

## 4. BRAND TERMS GLOSSARY

Every article must use these terms consistently. Definitions are binding.

### AX (Agent Experience)
The practice of designing, evaluating, and optimizing the interactions between humans and autonomous AI agents. Broader than UX (which focuses on human-computer interaction) and conversation design (which focuses on dialogue). AX encompasses how agents behave, how they communicate, how they handle uncertainty, and how they earn trust.

### Agent Experience Design (AX Design)
The discipline of creating interfaces and behaviors for AI agents that are useful, usable, and trustworthy from the user's perspective. AX designers work on agent behavior, output quality, feedback patterns, error recovery, and user control — not just visual screens.

### Agentic
Describing AI systems that act autonomously toward goals, rather than responding to discrete prompts. Agentic systems perceive, plan, act, and iterate. Not all AI is agentic. ChatGPT (in its standard form) is not agentic — it responds to prompts. An AI agent that autonomously plans and executes multi-step workflows is agentic.

### LLM (Large Language Model)
A neural network trained on vast text corpora that can generate human-like text. LLMs are the engine behind most current AI agents. They are not agents themselves — they are the reasoning component that agents use. LLMs predict tokens. Agents use LLMs to take actions.

### Prompt Design
The practice of crafting inputs that guide an LLM's output toward a desired result. It sits between writing and engineering. Prompt designers think about structure, constraints, examples, and context — much like UX designers think about user inputs.

### Conversation Design
The practice of designing the dialogue between users and AI systems. Includes turn-taking, error recovery, tone, and escalation. Broader than chatbot design — conversation design applies to voice assistants, agentic workflows, and hybrid human-AI interactions.

### UX (User Experience)
The practice of designing products that are useful, usable, and desirable from the user's perspective. Traditional UX focuses on human-computer interaction: screens, flows, interfaces, and usability testing.

### Design System
A collection of reusable components, guided by consistent standards, that developers and designers can use to assemble applications. In the AX era, design systems must account for non-deterministic AI outputs — a fundamental shift from traditional component-based systems.

## 5. AEO WRITING RULES

AEO = Answer Engine Optimization. Writing so that AI engines (ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, etc.) will cite our content as the authoritative answer.

### The 50-Word Opening Answer

Every article must start with a direct, self-contained answer to the article's core question. This is the block that AI engines will extract and display. Rules:

- **Exactly 1–3 sentences.**
- **No introductory fluff.** No "Agent Experience design refers to..." — start with the definition.
- **Complete on its own.** A reader should understand the core concept after reading just this block.
- **Written in plain language.** If a 10th grader can't understand it, rewrite it.

**Example (correct):**
> "Agent Experience (AX) design is the practice of designing how humans interact with autonomous AI agents. It covers agent behavior, output quality, feedback patterns, and error recovery — going beyond traditional interface design to shape what AI agents actually do."

**Example (wrong):**
> "In today's evolving tech landscape, the concept of Agent Experience has emerged as a critical discipline. In this article, we'll explore what AX design means and how it differs from UX."

### FAQ Section Structure

- **4–6 questions per article** (2 for quick answers, 6 for pillar posts)
- **Each question is a complete sentence** in natural language.
- **Each answer is 40–80 words.** Direct. No filler.
- **Questions mirror real search queries.** Use Google autocomplete, People Also Ask, and Reddit questions as source material.
- **Format for MDX:** Use the FAQPage schema. Each FAQ is a separate entry with question and answer.

### Writing for AI Citation

- **Define terms explicitly.** AI engines pull definitions from clearly structured "X is Y" statements.
- **Use comparative language.** "Unlike UX design, AX focuses on..." — AI engines love comparative structures.
- **Include data points.** Numbers, percentages, and specific figures are prioritized by AI engines as authoritative.
- **Avoid ambiguity.** "It depends" kills citations. Take a position, then qualify it.
- **Link internally.** AI engines crawl internal links to understand topic relationships. Every article links to 3+ other articles.

## 6. AEO OPENING ANSWER FORMULA

Every article starts with this pattern:

```
[Direct definition or answer in 1–3 sentences. No intro. No context-setting. Just the answer.]

[Transition paragraph: Why this matters now. One sentence.]
```

**Template for the opening answer:**

```
[Term] is [category] + [what it does] + [how it's different from what came before].

[Optional: One-sentence elaboration or key detail.]
```

**Filled example:**

> "Prompt design is the practice of crafting structured inputs that guide AI language models toward reliable, useful outputs. It sits between writing and engineering — prompt designers think about constraints, examples, and context the way UX designers think about user flows."

Before every article is published, verify:

- [ ] MDX frontmatter is complete (title, slug, date, keywords, pillar, type, meta description, OG image)
- [ ] FAQPage schema is included in frontmatter
- [ ] Opening answer is 50 words or fewer and stands alone
- [ ] No banned phrases used
- [ ] At least 3 internal links included
- [ ] At least 1 external source cited
- [ ] Newsletter CTA at the bottom
- [ ] Title is H1, headings are H2/H3 in proper hierarchy
- [ ] Word count meets the type minimum (Quick Answer: 600+, Pillar: 2,500+)
- [ ] No "dive in," "unlock," "empower," "journey," or "seamless"
- [ ] Meta description is 140–160 characters
- [ ] Title tag is 50–60 characters
